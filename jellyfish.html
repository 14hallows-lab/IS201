Jellyfish

Ancient, alien, and utterly hypnotic — jellyfish have drifted through Earth's oceans for over 500 million years, long before the dinosaurs existed.

Dive deeper ↓

Wild Facts

Jellyfish are not fish at all — they're medusae, free-swimming members of the phylum Cnidaria. They have no brain, no heart, no bones, and are made of roughly 95% water. Yet they are remarkably effective predators.

  1. Body & Biology
    • No brain, heart, or blood — only a simple nerve net
    • Three layers: epidermis, mesoglea (jelly), and gastrodermis
    • Mouth and anus are the same opening
    • Some species are biologically immortal (Turritopsis dohrnii)
  2. Movement & Hunting
    • Propel themselves by rhythmically contracting their bell
    • Drift with ocean currents to conserve energy
    • Stinging cells (nematocysts) fire in under 700 nanoseconds
    • Tentacles can extend several meters to catch prey
  3. Ecology & Significance
    • Key food source for sea turtles, tuna, and ocean sunfish
    • "Blooms" of millions can clog power plant intakes
    • Some glow bioluminescently — GFP (green fluorescent protein) was discovered in jellyfish
    • Climate change is causing more frequent and larger blooms worldwide
  4. Human Uses
    • Eaten as a delicacy in China, Japan, and Korea
    • GFP used as a marker in biomedical research
    • Collagen studied for wound-healing applications
    • Inspiring soft-robotics designs in engineering

Notable Species

Moon Jellyfish

Aurelia aurita — the most common species worldwide. Translucent, with four visible gonads in a clover pattern.

Lion's Mane

Cyanea capillata — the largest jellyfish on Earth. Its tentacles can exceed 36 meters in length.

Box Jellyfish

Chironex fleckeri — has 24 eyes and is considered the most venomous marine animal known.

Immortal Jellyfish

Turritopsis dohrnii — can revert to its juvenile polyp state after reaching adulthood, cycling indefinitely.

Cannonball Jellyfish

Stomolophus meleagris — a dome-shaped species common in the Gulf of Mexico and Atlantic coast.

Spotted Jelly

Mastigias papua — carries symbiotic algae that photosynthesize to help feed the jellyfish.
